Lead Researcher
Understanding Visual Perception in Virtual Reality
Virtual RealityExperimental DesignPsychophysicsData AnalysisComputational Modeling
+

Overview

This project explored lightness constancy — our ability to recognise the true appearance of a surface despite changes in lighting — and whether more realistic VR environments improve perceptual accuracy.

Approach

I designed VR experiments in custom Unity environments, manipulating surface orientation, lighting direction, material properties, shadows, and environmental context. Participants completed perceptual matching tasks analysed using computational models.

Key Findings

Realistic visual cues improved surface judgments, but performance in even highly detailed VR remained substantially below real-world levels, suggesting perception in VR differs from physical environments in ways not yet fully understood.

Impact

Findings have implications for VR design in training, simulation, education, and healthcare applications where visual realism is critical.

Understanding How People Make Decisions Under Uncertainty
Computational ModelingPsychophysicsStatistical AnalysisHuman Decision-Making
+

Overview

This project investigated how people make perceptual decisions when there is no perfectly certain answer — and whether they always choose the most likely option or distribute choices according to perceived probabilities.

Approach

Using computational models and behavioural experiments, we compared theories of perceptual decision-making. Participants completed visual tasks under uncertain conditions, and responses were compared to predictions from competing models.

Key Findings

Experienced observers were more consistent and efficient than probability-matching theories predict, suggesting human perception often operates closer to an optimal decision-making strategy.

Impact

Findings have implications for psychology, neuroscience, AI, and any system relying on human judgment under uncertainty.

Lead Researcher
Making Digital Surfaces Look Real
Perception ResearchExperimental DesignPsychophysicsDisplay TechnologyHuman Factors
+

Overview

Although modern displays render highly realistic images, people can usually distinguish a physical object from something on a screen. This project investigated which visual cues drive that distinction and whether displays can be made to resemble physical materials like paper.

Approach

I conducted experiments comparing physical paper samples with digital versions on an LCD screen, manipulating colour consistency and surface texture cues. Participants judged whether surfaces appeared real or virtual under different conditions.

Key Findings

Colour was the strongest cue. Matching the display's chromaticity to surrounding lighting made digital surfaces substantially more realistic. Physical textures improved realism more than digitally simulated ones.

Impact

Provides practical guidance for creating convincing digital displays and established the foundation for later VR perception research.

Lead Researcher
Bridging Real and Virtual Perception
Lightness PerceptionDisplay CalibrationPsychophysicsExperimental DesignHuman Factors
+

Overview

Building on earlier display research, this project investigated whether carefully calibrated digital displays could produce lightness perception equivalent to physical paper surfaces — enabling more flexible experimental methods without sacrificing perceptual realism.

Approach

Participants completed lightness-matching tasks comparing real paper with LCD-displayed patches. I manipulated colour calibration, texture cues, and physical vs. digital presentation to isolate which factors were necessary for realistic perception.

Key Findings

Colour calibration was the critical factor. When chromaticity matched the surrounding environment, lightness judgments closely resembled those with physical surfaces. Texture contributed less than expected.

Impact

Provides a practical framework for researchers and designers creating virtual environments that support realistic visual judgments.
